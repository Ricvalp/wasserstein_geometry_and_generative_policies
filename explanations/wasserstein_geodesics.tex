\title{Proof That Displacement Interpolation Gives Wasserstein Geodesics}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

Assume $\mu,\nu\in\mathcal P_2(\mathbb R^d)$ and that the optimal transport for
the quadratic cost is induced by a Brenier map
\[
T:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d,
\qquad
T_\#\mu=\nu.
\]
For the quadratic cost, define
\[
T_t(x)=(1-t)x+tT(x),
\qquad t\in[0,1],
\]
and
\[
\mu_t=(T_t)_\#\mu.
\]
This curve moves each particle along the straight line from $x$ to $T(x)$.

\section*{Claim}

The curve $(\mu_t)_{t\in[0,1]}$ is a constant-speed geodesic:
\[
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
=
|t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu),
\qquad
s,t\in[0,1].
\]

\section*{Proof}

Fix $0\le s<t\le 1$. Consider the coupling
\[
\gamma_{s,t}=(T_s,T_t)_\#\mu
\in \Pi(\mu_s,\mu_t).
\]
This coupling pairs the position at time $s$ and the position at time $t$ of the
same original particle $x$. For each such particle,
\[
|T_t(x)-T_s(x)|
=
|(t-s)(T(x)-x)|
=
|t-s|\,|T(x)-x|.
\]
Using $\gamma_{s,t}$ as an admissible transport plan gives
\[
W_2^2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
\le
\int_{\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d}|a-b|^2\,d\gamma_{s,t}(a,b)
=
|t-s|^2
\int_{\mathbb R^d}|T(x)-x|^2\,d\mu(x).
\]
Since $T$ is optimal from $\mu$ to $\nu$,
\[
\int_{\mathbb R^d}|T(x)-x|^2\,d\mu(x)
=
W_2^2(\mu,\nu).
\]
Thus
\[
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
\le
|t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).
\]

It remains to prove the reverse inequality. Apply the same upper bound on the
intervals $[0,s]$ and $[t,1]$:
\[
W_2(\mu,\mu_s)
\le
s\,W_2(\mu,\nu),
\qquad
W_2(\mu_t,\nu)
\le
(1-t)\,W_2(\mu,\nu).
\]
By the triangle inequality,
\[
W_2(\mu,\nu)
\le
W_2(\mu,\mu_s)
+
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
+
W_2(\mu_t,\nu).
\]
Therefore
\[
W_2(\mu,\nu)
\le
sW_2(\mu,\nu)
+
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
+
(1-t)W_2(\mu,\nu).
\]
Rearranging,
\[
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
\ge
|t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).
\]
Combining the upper and lower bounds gives
\[
W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)
=
|t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).
\]
This is exactly the constant-speed geodesic condition.

\section*{Interpretation}

Euclidean geodesics move points along straight lines. Wasserstein geodesics move
mass along optimal straight-line particle trajectories. This is why the curve
$\mu_t=(T_t)_\#\mu$ is called displacement interpolation.
