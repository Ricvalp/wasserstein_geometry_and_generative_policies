wasserstein geodesics

Assume \(\mu,\nu\in\mathcal P_2(\mathbb R^d)\) and that the optimal transport for the quadratic cost is induced by a Brenier map \[T:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d, \qquad T_\#\mu=\nu.\] For the quadratic cost, define \[T_t(x)=(1-t)x+tT(x), \qquad t\in[0,1],\] and \[\mu_t=(T_t)_\#\mu.\] This curve moves each particle along the straight line from \(x\) to \(T(x)\).

Claim

The curve \((\mu_t)_{t\in[0,1]}\) is a constant-speed geodesic: \[W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) = |t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu), \qquad s,t\in[0,1].\]

Proof

Fix \(0\le s<t\le 1\). Consider the coupling \[\gamma_{s,t}=(T_s,T_t)_\#\mu \in \Pi(\mu_s,\mu_t).\] This coupling pairs the position at time \(s\) and the position at time \(t\) of the same original particle \(x\). For each such particle, \[|T_t(x)-T_s(x)| = |(t-s)(T(x)-x)| = |t-s|\,|T(x)-x|.\] Using \(\gamma_{s,t}\) as an admissible transport plan gives \[W_2^2(\mu_s,\mu_t) \le \int_{\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d}|a-b|^2\,d\gamma_{s,t}(a,b).\] We now rewrite this integral in terms of the original variable \(x\). Define \[F_{s,t}(x)=(T_s(x),T_t(x)).\] By definition, \[\gamma_{s,t}=(F_{s,t})_\#\mu.\] The defining property of a pushforward measure says that, for every integrable function \(\Phi:\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R\), \[\int_{\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d} \Phi(a,b)\,d\gamma_{s,t}(a,b) = \int_{\mathbb R^d} \Phi(F_{s,t}(x))\,d\mu(x).\] Here we choose \[\Phi(a,b)=|a-b|^2.\] Therefore \[\int_{\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d}|a-b|^2\,d\gamma_{s,t}(a,b) = \int_{\mathbb R^d}|T_s(x)-T_t(x)|^2\,d\mu(x).\] This is why the integral over \(\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d\) becomes an integral over \(\mathbb R^d\): the coupling \(\gamma_{s,t}\) was itself obtained by pushing the original measure \(\mu\) forward through the map \(x\mapsto (T_s(x),T_t(x))\).

Now compute the integrand: \[T_t(x)-T_s(x) = ((1-t)x+tT(x))-((1-s)x+sT(x)).\] The terms involving \(x\) and \(T(x)\) collect as \[T_t(x)-T_s(x) = (s-t)x+(t-s)T(x) = (t-s)(T(x)-x).\] Hence \[|T_t(x)-T_s(x)|^2 = |t-s|^2\,|T(x)-x|^2.\] Substituting this into the previous integral gives \[\int_{\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d}|a-b|^2\,d\gamma_{s,t}(a,b) = |t-s|^2 \int_{\mathbb R^d}|T(x)-x|^2\,d\mu(x).\] Thus \[W_2^2(\mu_s,\mu_t) \le |t-s|^2 \int_{\mathbb R^d}|T(x)-x|^2\,d\mu(x).\] Since \(T\) is optimal from \(\mu\) to \(\nu\), \[\int_{\mathbb R^d}|T(x)-x|^2\,d\mu(x) = W_2^2(\mu,\nu).\] Thus \[W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) \le |t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).\]

It remains to prove the reverse inequality. Apply the same upper bound on the intervals \([0,s]\) and \([t,1]\): \[W_2(\mu,\mu_s) \le s\,W_2(\mu,\nu), \qquad W_2(\mu_t,\nu) \le (1-t)\,W_2(\mu,\nu).\] By the triangle inequality, \[W_2(\mu,\nu) \le W_2(\mu,\mu_s) + W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) + W_2(\mu_t,\nu).\] Therefore \[W_2(\mu,\nu) \le sW_2(\mu,\nu) + W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) + (1-t)W_2(\mu,\nu).\] Rearranging, \[W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) \ge |t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).\] Combining the upper and lower bounds gives \[W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t) = |t-s|\,W_2(\mu,\nu).\] This is exactly the constant-speed geodesic condition.

Interpretation

Euclidean geodesics move points along straight lines. Wasserstein geodesics move mass along optimal straight-line particle trajectories. This is why the curve \(\mu_t=(T_t)_\#\mu\) is called displacement interpolation.
